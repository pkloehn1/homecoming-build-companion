## Summary

- A converter-economics master class: how to use enhancement converters to turn cheap recipes/IOs into the exact pieces you want, organized by the three conversion modes — **in set** (3 converters, random different member of same set), **by category** (2 converters, random member of a different set in the same category), and **by rarity** (1 converter, random IO in a different set of the same rarity). Yomo's heuristic value of a converter is ~66,666 inf.
- Walks every conversion sub-economy in turn — Winter-Os (~16.5 converters average to target a specific WO; Winter Packs are the supply hose), PvP IOs (Panacea/Shield Wall/Glad Armor procs are auto-include in every build; bid lvl-10 sniper-set recipes and convert), purples (use lvl-50 Soulbound Allegiance D/R as universal seed; ~28 converters per piece average to target one purple), ATOs (use category to randomize within an AT pair; lvl-50 Command of the Mastermind A/D/E/R is the seed for Scrapper's Strike), and the regular IO sets walked alphabetically (Accurate Defense Debuff, Accurate Healing, Accurate To-Hit Debuff, Confuse, Defense Debuff, Defense, Endurance Modification…).
- Defense is the proverbial "money shot": the LotG +recharge proc is the single most valuable IO in the game, and the entire defense category is structured to funnel into it. Yomo's standard slotting on Combat Jumping is four procs — Kismet +ToHit, Reactive Defenses scaling-resist, Shield Wall +res, LotG +recharge.
- Targets specific level ranges per category to minimize conversion fodder: lvl 10–14 for Karma/Kismet (no rare salvage, Kismet proc is the highest-value piece in that band); lvl 31–40 for Serendipity → GotA fodder; lvl 41–49 for Red Fortune/Reactive Defenses → LotG (avoid lvl 50 because crafting cost doubles vs lvl 49). Resetting an IO's level via /AH (sell at one level, rebuy at another for −10% fees) is a recommended advanced tactic.
- Probability primer: averages converge over many conversions but variance dominates any single attempt; converting in a 6-member set is "like rolling a d6 and discarding a result equal to what you started with." Streaks of 5+ identical results are normal and predicted by the Law of Large Numbers, not a bug.

### Post 1 — Section: Intro & three conversion modes

I'm using "Ultimate" here not necessarily in terms of "best" but in terms of "last"; this will certainly be my last guide on the matter, if not my last guide entirely. Do you like converters? Do you like math? Too bad, because this is basically a guide on probability and how to use converters to get exactly what you want.

This is not intended as an inf-making guide, although it is how I and many other made the lion's share of our stashes. This is intended as a "What happens when the grid goes down and the zombies take over (GGD;ZTO) and no one else is there to supply me the things I want in the /AH?" guide.

Enhancement converters can be used to change, or convert if you will, one enhancement into another. There are basically three ways they can do this:

1. You can convert an enhancement IN SET to a random different enhancement in the same set at a cost of three converters;
2. You can convert an enhancement into a random enhancement in a different random set of the same RARITY (like Rare IO, or PvP IO) at a cost of one converter. "Rarity" might cause some confusion because regular invention sets can be classified as "uncommon" or "rare". But each rarity is an independent set;
3. You can convert some enhancements into a random enhancement in a different random set of the same CATEGORY (like a resistance IO of the same level but in a different set, or a scrapper ATO into the other scrapper ATO set) at the cost of two converters.

My heuristic is to value [converters] at 66,666 inf apiece.

A few things to note:

- You will get back something different each time you convert. So if you are converting in set of a set with six members, there are only five outcomes (or N−1, where N is the number of elements in the set). It's like rolling a d6 die and throwing out the result if you get what you already have. You can (and will) get back what you started with if you keep rolling.
- You will only get back something in the same class/rarity. "Regular" set IOs are in a different set from PvP IOs, e.g., and you will never get a Shield Wall defense PvP IO when you start with a Kismet.
- You will only get back something in the same level range. Many IOs have a level printed right on the icon, but anything level-attuned is fine.

You can acquire converters in a few ways: using a merit vendor to convert reward merits to converters, or by buying them outright from someone else on the /AH.

I'm going to cover various enhancements by rarity: Winter-Os; PvP IOs; purple IOs; ATOs; uncommon and rare invention set IOs. These are five distinct categories and you cannot convert from one of these rarities into another. That said, you can convert WITHIN these rarities willy nilly and pell mell.

It may seem strange to start with the most expensive sets, but I hope you will see that is because they are smaller and simpler groups which should help demonstrate the math and logic more easily.

### Post 2 — Section: Winter-Os

Note that all WOs come in attuned form only. At level 50, you can use a catalyst to change the WO into Superior form, which enables better set bonuses. WOs and Superior WOs are considered different sets, and although you can change a WO to a Superior WO, you cannot change a Superior WO to a regular WO.

Sources:

1. Winter Packs, 25mm inf per pack, 1.2 random WOs on average + other stuff. That "other stuff" can include converters and merits (which I turn straight into converters). All items are put in your Character Items menu, accessible by every character on your account at any time.
2. 100 merits at merit vendor, can select exact WO desired. Economically viable.
3. /AH. You can buy the specific item you are looking for if someone else has posted it at your price or below. You can buy a different WO in the set and convert it into the piece you want (average of 15 converters).

There are five total WO sets: Entomb (Hold); Blistering Cold (Melee); Avalanche (Melee AoE); Winter's Bite (Ranged); Frozen Blast (Ranged AoE).

Test case math: 24 rolls by rarity for 6 Blistering Cold gets us 4 unique members of the set and 2 duplicates, so reroll 2 of them until you have 6 unique members and a full set. 13 rolls in set. 63 converters in total, or average of 10.5 converters per enhancement.

An average of 16.5 converters means it generally costs ~1mm to change ANY WO into the exact one you want. The real value is in opening Winter Packs — every pack averages 1.2 WOs and ~9 merits (which becomes 27 converters). Why dump WOs you don't want in the /AH when you can change them into the exact ones you do want?

I usually find Blistering Cold and Avalanche have the most demand, followed by Winter's Bite, Frozen Blast, and finally Entomb. I 5-slot Blistering Cold in melee for slow resist (2 pieces) and defense (5 pieces); I 5-slot Avalanche in a melee AoE; Winter's Bite is worthwhile for ranged defense, but slow protection doesn't kick in until 3 pieces; Frozen Blast isn't a great set, but two pieces gets you slow protection.

### Post 3 — Section: Probability primer

Probability distributions have two major components: average and spread (mean and variance/standard deviation).

The mean tells you that if you do something many many times, it will tend towards that number. When I say it will take 5 rolls on average to get from one member of a six-member set to a specific IO, one attempt may take you one roll, or it may take you a thousand rolls. But if you do this with 100 IOs, it will probably take around 500 total rolls. The average is not a guarantee, but over the long run it works out close.

The variance/spread tells you how wide a path there may be. A higher variance means more variability in the outcome.

### Post 4 — Section: PvP IOs

PvP IOs can either have a visible level attached, or they can be attuned. PvP IOs work with their set bonuses **all the way down** — if you have several lvl 50 PvP IOs in the same set slotted in a power, those set bonuses will work even if you exemplar to a lower level. There are two types of set bonuses: regular (always in effect) and PvP (only in effect when you are in PvP).

In general, I slot attuned PvP IOs until I hit level 50. At level 50 I unslot them and replace them with level 50s of the same set, plus +5 boosters where applicable, since I get more enhancement and the set bonuses still work down on exemplaring.

You can convert a PvP IO by rarity into any specific PvP IO in a different set. Volatility increases as the number of possible outcomes increases.

Test case (less efficient): Create full set of Shield Wall from lvl 10 crafted Javelin Volley A/E/R, one at a time:

- Shield Wall D: 3 by rarity to SW E/R; 1 in set; total 6 converters
- Shield Wall D/E: 8 by rarity to SW proc; 5 in set; total 23 converters
- Shield Wall D/E/R: 7 by rarity to SW proc; 1 in set; total 10 converters
- Shield Wall D/R: 2 by rarity to SW D/E; 1 in set; total 5 converters
- Shield Wall E/R: 3 by rarity to SW proc; 2 in set; total 9 converters
- Shield Wall proc: 12 by rarity to SW D/E/R; 1 in set; total 15 converters
  68 total converters, average 11.33 per piece. Lucky run.

Test case (more efficient): Full set of Panacea from lvl 10 crafted JV A/E/R, all to Panacea, then reroll duplicates until six unique IOs. (Generally beats one-at-a-time targeting.)

Every character I run has at least three PvP IOs (Panacea proc, Shield Wall proc, Glad Armor proc) and potentially many more. Those are usually 8–10mm each on the /AH. There are also PLENTY of "garbage" PvP IOs available crafted or as recipes for much cheaper. Buy three random PvP IOs for 5mm apiece, convert them into the three I need for ~1mm extra in converters, win.

I bid on lvl 10 recipes of low-demand types like ranged damage. When I buy a block of 30–50, I'll craft and convert into Panacea, Shield Wall, some Glad Armor, maybe keep procs along the way.

PvP IOs are also great for frankenslotting. Their level range is 10–50, so you don't need to think about replacing an attuned until you want to.

### Post 6 — Section: ATOs

ATOs are always attuned, and you can slot any ATO of your archetype starting at level 10. You cannot slot ATOs not of your archetype. Once you hit level 50, use a catalyst to improve a slotted ATO to Superior ASAP — better set bonuses and procs. At 50, catalysts drop with an 18-hour timer; I always buy them from /AH so I don't wait.

Each AT has two different ATO sets, so we are introducing "category." Since there are only two sets per AT, every time you convert one ATO by category you're guaranteed a random member of the other set.

A good strategy is to split a set 3-3 or even 2-2-2 for the set bonus. "Superior" is considered a different set than regular ATO, so 3 Superior + 3 regular gives both 3-piece bonuses.

Sources:

1. 100 merits for an attuned ATO recipe at the merit vendor.
2. Hero packs or Vigilante packs, 10mm apiece on /AH. Each averages 1.2 random ATOs and lots of other stuff including ~10 merits.
3. /AH — buy any ATO attuned.

Test case (less efficient) — full Mark of the Tanker / Gauntleted Fist by piece:

- 8 by rarity to MotT; 1 by category to GF D/R; 25 in set; total 85 converters
- GF D/E/R: 6 by rarity to GF A/D; 10 in set; total 36 converters
- GF A/D/E/R: 7 by rarity to GF A/D/R; 6 in set; total 25 converters
- GF proc: 23 by rarity to MotT; 1 by category to GF D/R; 1 in set; total 28 converters
  245 total, average 40.83 per piece. Below-average rolls.

Test case (more efficient) — full Scrapper's Strike from lvl 50 Command of the Mastermind A/D/E/R, all to SS, then reroll: 79 total converters, average 13.17 per piece.

If you are a scrapper and want two full sets of SS, you are often better off buying on /AH. You can still profit from buying hero/vigilante packs, selling expensive ATOs, converting cheap ones — but the price spread between expensive and cheap ATOs isn't large now, so margin is thin.

Niche use: because each ATO is one or two members in a category set, converting by category randomizes you to the other set. Useful when you have large numbers of stuff.

### Post 7 — Section: IO sets — three conversion modes restated

IOs can be categorized and converted in three ways:

1. **By set.** Each set has between 3 and 6 members; three converters convert in-set to a different random member.
2. **By category.** Each IO is a member of a category, like Defense or Universal Travel. Two converters convert into a random IO of a different set in the same category, if a different set is available.
3. **By rarity.** Two rarities: Uncommon and Rare. One converter gets you an IO in the same rarity but in a different set.

### Post 9 — Section: Reply re: streaks (March 22)

> Do you ever get those odd occurrences where you're converting an unattuned enhancement and you only go back and forth between two sets, even when there are other sets available at that level?

Happens all the time. You really notice it when it doesn't work the way you want, but people tend to overlook it when they get what they want pretty quickly.

If you flip a coin until you get a heads, about one in a thousand times you will flip tails ten times in a row. On average it'll take two flips, but you don't hold onto the memory of getting it on the first try. You remember the ten failures.

More detail: lvl 50 Aegis converted by Resistance category — at that level there are only three sets in category (Aegis, Titanium Coating, Unbreakable Guard). If you are lucky… [the bouncing back-and-forth between two of the three is just how the RNG looks in a small candidate pool].

### Post 13 — Section: Confuse (and mez-set structure overall)

Befuddling Aura (lvl 10–30, uncommon, 5 members); Cacophony (lvl 10–30, rare, 6 members); Perplex (lvl 20–50, uncommon, 5 members); Malaise's Illusions (lvl 20–50, rare, 6 members). Purple: Coercive Persuasion (lvl 50, 6 members).

Generally, the mez sets (Confuse, Fear, Hold\*, Immobilize, Sleep, Stun) are structured the same: a low-level set pair (uncommon/rare) and a high-level set pair (uncommon/rare), all four overlapping at lvl 20–30. If targeting one of the rare sets, do it OUTSIDE the overlap range — converting a crafted Befuddling Aura to a Cacophony at lvl 10–19 takes one guaranteed conversion by category.

Inside the lvl 20–30 overlap, three out of four sets you'd land on after conversion are uncommon — annoying. So I try to avoid mez sets in that range, focusing on under 20 or over 30.

Confuse is underrated as a mez type — it breaks aggro and gets opponents off your back. They won't run away since they want to attack their neighbor. Damage to their neighbor can be great or annoying.

Set bonuses on the four basic IO sets are meh until 5 pieces (some recharge). Procs for Cacophony and Malaise are Energy Damage and Psionic Damage respectively.

If I have a character that uses Confuse a lot, I'll often 5-slot Coercive Persuasion when I hit 50 and definitely use the proc. Pre-50 I'd 5-slot Malaise attuned. Accuracy and recharge are more important to me than mez duration. Good value at 50: two-slot Coercive Persuasion A/R 50+5 and the proc.

### Post 14 — Section: Defense Debuffs

Exploited Vulnerability (lvl 10–20, uncommon, 3 pieces); Achilles' Heel (lvl 10–20, rare, 3 pieces); Undermined Defenses (lvl 21–50, uncommon, 6 pieces); Touch of Lady Grey (lvl 21–50, rare, 6 pieces).

I've never slotted a power to enhance its ability to debuff defense. Most enemies don't last more than a hit or two and accuracy/+to hit are easy to come by. All set bonuses in this category are pretty much garbage. The one shining star is the **Achilles' Heel %-Resistance proc**.

Easy way to get an Achilles' proc without buying it: start with an Exploited Vulnerability recipe or IO, convert once by category, then convert in set until you get the Achilles proc. Average ~6 converters total. Or start with an Achilles' non-proc and convert in set for ~6 converters average.

This is one reason Exploited Vulnerability recipes/enhancements are pricier than their usefulness suggests, and why the Achilles' proc has dropped in price. The rest of the category is conversion fodder.

Karma (lvl 10–30, uncommon, 3-piece); Kismet (lvl 10–30, rare, 5-piece); Serendipity (lvl 15–40, uncommon, 6-piece); Gift of the Ancients (lvl 15–40, rare, 6-piece); Reactive Defenses (lvl 20–50, rare, 6-piece); Red Fortune (lvl 25–50, uncommon, 6-piece); Luck of the Gambler (lvl 25–50, rare, 6-piece). This category is the proverbial money shot. Every defense recipe and enhancement is valuable, mainly because the LotG proc is so insanely useful.

Standard defense slotting for a defense/endurance toggle is usually three [pieces].

**Karma:** for a low-level character as placeholder. The −KB proc is the valuable in-set piece — 4 points of knockback protection as early as level 7 has real value. **Serendipity:** Six-piece uncommon, lvl 15–40. I have never slotted a single piece. No proc, set bonuses negligible. Pure conversion fodder — defense set that doesn't require rare salvage. That's why it's relatively high-priced in /AH despite being unslotted by anyone.

**Gift of the Ancients:** Slightly better set bonuses than Serendipity; proc is +run speed (I tried it on a speedster). I find better alternatives at every level — conversion fodder. One of six recipes doesn't require rare salvage.

**Red Fortune:** Uncommon counterpart to LotG. Reasonable set bonuses; the **only defense set with a set bonus that adds defense** to my knowledge. I always craft these and either sell directly (often to myself) or convert into LotG.

**Luck of the Gambler (LotG):** Six-piece rare, lvl 25–50. This is the mother lode of IO enhancement value due to the proc, which provides defense enhancement plus a 7.5% recharge bonus. Rule of Five means up to five LotG procs in a build. I will almost always convert the D/E/R, D/R, and E/R pieces into the proc or whatever comes up first among the proc, the D, and the D/E. Every recipe requires rare salvage; recipes are almost always more expensive than I think they should be.

**Shield Wall (PvP):** Six-piece. Contains a must-have IO in the proc (damage resistance). When using more than one in a power, I place it attuned when I first slot. At 50 (could be 47, but I usually forget) I unslot non-proc pieces and replace them with lvl 50+5 boosted pieces — PvP IO set bonuses work all the way down.

### Post 17 — Section: Defense, part deux — level targeting

The trick is figuring out where "good enough to use/sell" is for you. Are you collecting recipes and crafting (focus on lowest levels for crafting cost)? Or did you get a defense IO from rare roulette?

**lvl 10–14:** Karma, Kismet. Convert by category gives 50/50 odds and on average 2 conversions (4 converters) to your target. Levels matter for which recipes don't require rare salvage. This is where I target crafting Karma and Kismet.

**lvl 15–19:** Karma, Kismet, Serendipity, GotA. Converting by category gives 1-in-3 chance and on average 3 rolls at 2 converters each. Crafting still cheap, but Kismet proc is the highest value here.

**lvl 20–24:** Karma, Kismet, Serendipity, GotA, Reactive Defenses. Three uncommon, two rare. Reactive Defenses proc is always a winner; D and D/E pieces sell well. 1-in-4 chance per category roll.

**lvl 25–30:** All seven defense sets in play.

**lvl 31–40:** [Mid-band where you target Serendipity/GotA crafting.]

**lvl 41–49:** Reactive Defenses, Red Fortune, LotG. With only three sets, 50/50 chance from RD or RF to LotG. **This is where I target crafting Red Fortune and Reactive Defenses.**

**lvl 50:** Same as 41–49 but crafting cost for a lvl 50 recipe is **twice** that of a lvl 49. Avoid crafting at level 50 — sell lvl 50 recipes.

**Advanced tactic — resetting levels:** You can sell an IO at one level and rebuy it on a different character at a different level thanks to fungibility/bucketing. You lose 10% to /AH, but you can improve odds enough that on average you make up that cost in fewer converters.

I do this ALL THE TIME. I have many characters and several SG bases dedicated to this in the defense complex. It takes practice, bulk, and confidence that no market PvPer is going to notice.

Quick defense note: I usually have Combat Jumping on most characters, four-slotted with Kismet proc, Reactive Defenses proc, Shield Wall proc, LotG proc. I cannot get better value than that.

### Post 20 — Section: How to acquire any given enhancement (the five paths)

This is a discussion I should have had earlier and may end up making a part of an earlier post: usually there are multiple ways to get to a given enhancement. If you are looking to pick up one or many, it's good to think about how to get where you want.

1. **Buy the enhancement outright on the /AH.** Things like HOs and DSOs only come from in-content drops or /AH purchase.
2. **Acquire the exact recipe + salvage and craft.** Recipes that come in a level range are fungible/bucketed — you can buy a lvl 50 Air Burst recipe at the same price as a lvl 10, and vice versa. Generally better off buying at the lowest crafting level.
3. **Acquire the exact recipe (any way) and craft at the appropriate level**, then use it as-is.
4. **Acquire/craft a different enhancement in the same category at appropriate level**, convert by category until you're in the set you want, convert in set until you have the piece you want. Sometimes you find a lucrative piece along the way that wasn't your original goal.
5. **Acquire/craft a different enhancement of any sort in the overall rarity** (e.g., PvP or rare IO set) at appropriate level, convert by rarity until you get a category you like, then by category until you get a set you like, then in set until you get a piece you like. Often you'll find a lucrative piece along the way and shift directions — if you want a LotG and you get an Aegis on the way, you may sell it or transform it into a different Resist Damage.

This list is in order from easiest to most converter/time/labor intensive, and that correlates to most-to-least expensive in terms of inf cost. Do you want a Panacea proc? You can buy one for ~9mm. Do you want to make one yourself? You can spend 5mm on a recipe and 1mm of converters and probably get there. Or buy a different PvP IO for 4mm, sell it there and use the proceeds for your Panacea? Do you want to target the recipe or target the enhancement? All valid questions.
